EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Meridian Urban Partners Ltd (MUP) is pleased to present the Stage One Planning Document for the Auckland Central Urban Renewal Project (ACURP). This project represents a $340 million investment in the transformation of a 14-hectare brownfield site in the central Auckland area. The proposal outlines the development of mixed-use residential, commercial, and retail precincts designed to increase housing density, stimulate economic activity, and modernise Auckland's urban core.

The development will deliver approximately 1,200 new residential dwellings, 6,500 sqm of commercial floor space, and 2,200 sqm of retail and hospitality offerings. A central public park of 1.8 hectares is proposed as the green heart of the development, surrounded by high-density residential towers ranging from 14 to 28 storeys.

This document outlines the project rationale, site context, development programme, financial modelling, and stakeholder engagement plan. It has been prepared in accordance with standard resource management and planning requirements and is intended to support the resource consent application process.

SECTION 1: PROJECT RATIONALE AND OBJECTIVES

1.1 Background

Auckland is experiencing a significant housing shortage. Current projections from Auckland Council indicate a shortfall of over 40,000 dwellings over the next decade. The Auckland Central Urban Renewal Project is a direct response to this crisis, aiming to deliver high-quality, high-density housing at scale.

The site, formerly occupied by industrial infrastructure decommissioned in 2019, presents an exceptional development opportunity. It is located within 1.2 kilometres of central Auckland's commercial core, is well-served by existing transport infrastructure including the City Rail Link, and has been identified in Auckland's Unitary Plan as a priority intensification zone.

1.2 Project Objectives

The objectives of the Auckland Central Urban Renewal Project are as follows:

a) Deliver 1,200 new residential units ranging from studio apartments to four-bedroom family homes to address Auckland's housing shortfall.
b) Create approximately 450 permanent jobs in the commercial and retail precincts upon full completion.
c) Activate the brownfield site, eliminating existing contamination and transforming derelict land into productive urban space.
d) Deliver world-class public amenity through the central park, pedestrian walkways, cycle lanes, and activated street frontages.
e) Generate an estimated $280 million in economic activity during the construction phase and $48 million annually upon completion.
f) Achieve a minimum 4-star Green Star rating across all buildings, supporting Auckland's carbon reduction commitments.

1.3 Market Analysis

The central Auckland residential market has demonstrated consistent price appreciation of 6.8% per annum over the last five years, driven by strong population growth, constrained supply, and ongoing migration. The demand for high-density urban living is particularly strong among young professionals, downsizers, and international investors.

Our market research indicates that studios and one-bedroom units in the $450,000–$650,000 price range represent the highest demand segment. Two and three-bedroom units in the $750,000–$1.2 million range are also experiencing strong pre-sales interest. Four-bedroom family units priced at $1.5–$2.2 million are targeting the premium segment with strong offshore buyer interest.

SECTION 2: SITE ANALYSIS AND PLANNING CONTEXT

2.1 Site Description

The project site encompasses 14.2 hectares of brownfield land in the central Auckland area. The site is currently characterized by decommissioned warehousing, redundant industrial infrastructure, and surface-level contamination from historical chemical storage. A geotechnical and contamination assessment completed in late 2025 confirmed the site is suitable for remediation and development.

The site is bounded to the north by a major arterial road, to the east by light commercial activity, to the south by an established residential neighbourhood, and to the west by rail infrastructure associated with the City Rail Link.

2.2 Planning Framework

The site is zoned for high-density residential and mixed-use development under the Auckland Unitary Plan (Operative in Part). The proposed development is consistent with the intensification provisions of the National Policy Statement on Urban Development 2020, which prioritises the delivery of housing near existing infrastructure and services.

A pre-application meeting with Auckland Council's planning team confirmed that the development is of a type and scale that can be accommodated within the existing planning framework, subject to the satisfactory resolution of heritage, transport, and stormwater matters.

2.3 Heritage and Cultural Considerations

The site does not contain any registered historic heritage places as defined under the Historic Places Act 1993 or the Heritage New Zealand Pouhere Taonga Act 2014. A Heritage New Zealand impact assessment was completed in Q4 2025 and confirmed no scheduled items are present on or adjacent to the site.

The development team notes that a general obligations exist under the Resource Management Act in relation to the relationship of Māori with their ancestral lands. A desktop review of available GIS data was completed, and no waahi tapu (sacred sites) or sites of significance are recorded in the immediate project footprint.

Cultural impact will be further assessed during the resource consent process as required by standard regulatory procedures. MUP intends to write to relevant parties as part of the required notification process.

2.4 Contamination and Remediation

Historical use of the site for chemical manufacturing and storage between 1942 and 1987 has resulted in ground contamination including petroleum hydrocarbons and heavy metals. A Phase Two Environmental Site Assessment has been completed and a Remedial Action Plan (RAP) is being prepared. Estimated remediation costs are budgeted at $18.4 million, included within the overall project cost.

Remediation work is scheduled to commence in Q3 2026, pending resource consent approval, and is projected to take 14 months to complete.

SECTION 3: DEVELOPMENT DESIGN AND PROGRAMME

3.1 Design Vision

The design vision for the Auckland Central Urban Renewal Project is to create a vibrant, connected, and sustainable urban neighbourhood that attracts residents, workers, and visitors alike. The design language draws on Auckland's industrial heritage, with exposed concrete, steel detailing, and large glazed facades nodding to the site's former use while delivering thoroughly contemporary residential and commercial environments.

The central park — provisionally named "The Common" — is conceived as a fully programmable public space capable of hosting farmers' markets, outdoor performances, fitness activities, and community events. A water feature referencing the site's proximity to the Waitemata Harbour will serve as the centrepiece of the park.

The design team includes award-winning architecture firms with deep Auckland experience. Public realm and landscape design has been led by an internationally recognised landscape architecture practice.

3.2 Residential Component

The residential programme comprises:

- 320 studio apartments (avg. 38 sqm internal area)
- 480 one-bedroom apartments (avg. 54 sqm internal area)
- 280 two-bedroom apartments (avg. 78 sqm internal area)
- 96 three-bedroom apartments (avg. 105 sqm internal area)
- 24 four-bedroom penthouses and townhouses (avg. 186 sqm internal area)

All residential dwellings will meet or exceed the Healthy Homes Standards. A minimum of 18% of units will be accessible as per Building Code D1 requirements. Communal amenities including rooftop terraces, co-working lounges, gyms, and parcel lockers will be provided across each residential tower.

3.3 Commercial and Retail Component

A dedicated commercial hub of 6,500 sqm NLA will be positioned on the northern edge of the site with frontage to the arterial road. This component is designed to attract small to medium enterprises, creative industries, and professional services firms. Flexible floor plates ranging from 250 sqm to 1,800 sqm are proposed to accommodate a wide range of tenants.

The retail and hospitality precinct of 2,200 sqm NLA wraps the base of the residential towers, activating street frontages and providing day-round activity. A curated retail mix including a boutique supermarket, café and restaurant tenancies, health services, and personal services is targeted.

3.4 Construction Programme

The anticipated construction programme is as follows:

- Remediation and bulk earthworks: Q3 2026 – Q4 2027
- Tower A (28 storeys, 320 units): Q1 2027 – Q2 2029
- Tower B (22 storeys, 260 units): Q3 2027 – Q4 2029
- Tower C (18 storeys, 220 units): Q1 2028 – Q2 2030
- Podium and ground floor retail: Q2 2028 – Q4 2029
- Commercial hub: Q4 2027 – Q2 2029
- Public realm and park: Q1 2029 – Q4 2030
- Full practical completion: Q4 2030

SECTION 4: STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT PLAN

4.1 Engagement Philosophy

Meridian Urban Partners recognises the importance of keeping neighbours and the wider community informed about the development programme. We are committed to open, transparent communication throughout the project lifecycle. Our engagement approach is designed to ensure that affected parties have access to relevant information and have appropriate opportunities to provide feedback.

4.2 Key Stakeholder Groups

The following stakeholder groups have been identified as relevant to the ACURP:

- Auckland Council planning and consenting teams
- New Zealand Transport Agency (in relation to transport network impacts)
- Watercare Services (in relation to water and wastewater infrastructure)
- Auckland Transport (in relation to bus, train, and cycle network impacts)
- Immediately adjoining property owners and occupiers
- The wider neighbourhood residential community
- Heritage New Zealand Pouhere Taonga
- Local business associations
- Future residents and commercial tenants

4.3 Engagement Activities

A programme of engagement activities is planned across the project lifecycle:

a) Pre-Consent Phase:
- Public information evening at a local community venue (date TBC)
- Distribution of project information pamphlet to addresses within 500m of the site boundary
- Project website with FAQ and feedback form
- Written notification to identified parties as required under the Resource Management Act

b) Construction Phase:
- Construction Management Plan to include community liaison provisions
- Dedicated community liaison phone number and email address
- Monthly project newsletter distributed to registered stakeholders
- Formal complaints register

c) Post-Construction:
- Open Day event at practical completion
- Ongoing resident engagement programme through building management

4.4 Regulatory Notifications

As part of the resource consent process, MUP will discharge its notification obligations under the Resource Management Act 1991. Affected parties, including territorial authorities and network utility operators, will be notified in accordance with the Act's requirements. MUP will respond to all submissions received during the notification period.

SECTION 5: FINANCIAL OVERVIEW

5.1 Project Cost Summary

Total project development cost is estimated at $338.6 million (excl. GST), comprising:

- Land acquisition and holding costs: $48.2 million
- Remediation and earthworks: $18.4 million
- Construction — residential towers: $198.4 million
- Construction — commercial and retail: $24.6 million
- Public realm and park: $12.8 million
- Professional fees (design, legal, planning, project management): $19.7 million
- Marketing and sales: $8.4 million
- Contingency (5%): $16.1 million

5.2 Revenue Projections

Estimated gross development value upon full sell-down of the residential component and sale of the commercial hub is projected at $478 million (excl. GST), generating a gross development margin of approximately 29.4%.

Retail and hospitality tenancies will be retained as an income-producing asset, with a stabilised net income of approximately $3.2 million per annum from Year 3 onwards.

5.3 Funding Structure

The project will be funded through a combination of senior construction finance, mezzanine debt, and equity from MUP's capital partners. Full funding commitments are in place subject to satisfactory resource consent being granted. MUP's capital partners include a domestic institutional investor and two offshore private equity groups with established New Zealand real estate track records.

SECTION 6: RISK REGISTER

The following key risks have been identified and are being managed by the project team:

1. Consent Risk — Moderate: The resource consent process may attract objections from neighbouring properties or third parties. MUP's consenting team has extensive experience managing complex consent applications. Mitigation: Engage experienced RMA counsel early; consider pre-lodgement mediation if opposition is identified.

2. Contamination Risk — Moderate-High: Contamination may be more extensive than current assessments indicate. Mitigation: A full Phase Two assessment is underway; remediation budget includes a 20% contingency.

3. Construction Cost Inflation — High: The New Zealand construction market is experiencing significant cost pressures. Mitigation: Early contractor engagement and GMP contract structures to be pursued.

4. Interest Rate Risk — Moderate: Construction finance costs are subject to interest rate movements. Mitigation: Interest rate cap agreements to be put in place at financial close.

5. Sales Velocity Risk — Low-Moderate: Apartment pre-sales may be slower than projected. Mitigation: Engage leading residential marketing agencies early; consider offshore sales programme.

6. Planning Policy Risk — Low: Changes to national planning policy could affect the development parameters. Mitigation: Monitor policy developments and maintain relationships with Auckland Council planning team.

SECTION 7: CONCLUSION AND NEXT STEPS

The Auckland Central Urban Renewal Project represents a transformational development opportunity that will deliver meaningful housing supply, economic activity, and public amenity to central Auckland. Meridian Urban Partners is confident that the project can be delivered to the highest standards of design, sustainability, and community benefit.

The next steps for the project are:

1. Finalise geotechnical and contamination reports for inclusion in the resource consent application.
2. Complete the detailed design package to resource consent standard.
3. Lodge the resource consent application with Auckland Council.
4. Commence contractor procurement process.
5. Finalise construction finance facility.
6. Launch residential pre-sales programme.

MUP welcomes any questions or feedback regarding this proposal and looks forward to progressing the project in partnership with Auckland Council and the wider community.
