EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Irongate Resources Ltd (Irongate) is proposing to expand its existing iron ore operations at the Mount Jarra site in the Pilbara region of Western Australia. The Stage 3 expansion will extend the current pit boundaries by approximately 14km², increase annual production from 18Mtpa to 34Mtpa, and extend the project life by an estimated 22 years.

This Community Engagement and Social Impact Assessment (CESIA) has been prepared as part of Irongate's Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) submission to the Western Australian Department of Jobs, Tourism, Science and Innovation and the Commonwealth Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water.

The Pilbara region is home to a number of Aboriginal communities and groups with cultural, historical, and legal connections to the land and resources within and adjacent to the project area. This document outlines Irongate's approach to engaging with these groups, managing cultural heritage risks, and delivering social benefits to affected communities during the Stage 3 expansion.

SECTION 1: PROJECT DESCRIPTION

1.1 Location and Scope

The Mount Jarra operation is located approximately 180km south-east of Port Hedland in the Pilbara region of Western Australia. The project area encompasses pastoral lease land currently held by Irongate, Crown land, and areas subject to Native Title determinations.

The Stage 3 expansion proposes:
- Pit extension northward by 9.2km² and eastward by 4.8km²
- Establishment of a new waste rock facility (Area D)
- Extension of the existing haul road network (38km of new access roads)
- Construction of a second processing train
- Expansion of the accommodation camp from 850 to 1,600 capacity
- New water borefield development (four new bores, estimated yield 2,400ML/year)

1.2 Native Title Status

The project area intersects with two native title determinations:
- Banyjima People native title area (Federal Court determination, 2012) — approximately 68% of the expansion footprint
- Nyiyaparli People native title area (NNTT determination, 2017) — approximately 32% of the expansion footprint

Native Title Act 1993 obligations apply. Irongate is in the process of negotiating Indigenous Land Use Agreements (ILUAs) with the relevant Prescribed Bodies Corporate (PBCs) for both groups to provide certainty for the Stage 3 development.

1.3 Heritage Status

A desktop cultural heritage assessment has been completed by an external heritage consultancy. A total of 47 sites of potential heritage significance have been identified within 500m of the proposed expansion footprint, including rock art sites, stone artefact scatters, and ceremonial sites.

A Level 3 survey is planned for Q1 2026, to be conducted in accordance with the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2021 (WA).

SECTION 2: REGULATORY AND POLICY FRAMEWORK

2.1 Native Title Act 1993

The Native Title Act 1993 requires that Irongate comply with the Right to Negotiate provisions of the Future Act regime where the proposed activity may affect native title rights and interests. Future Act notices have been issued to the Banyjima PBC (registered as Banyjima Traditional Owners Prescribed Body Corporate) and the Nyiyaparli PBC (Nyiyaparli Yinhawangka Baiyungu Aboriginal Corporation RNTBC).

The six-month negotiation period commenced on 3 September 2025.

2.2 Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2021 (WA)

The Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2021 replaced the former Aboriginal Heritage Act 1972 and introduced significant changes to how cultural heritage is managed in Western Australia. Under the new Act, Irongate has developed a Cultural Heritage Management Plan (CHMP) in consultation with the relevant Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Consultation Bodies (ACHCBs).

The CHMP sets out procedures for:
- Identification and assessment of cultural heritage places
- Heritage surveys prior to ground disturbance
- Heritage monitoring during construction
- Reporting and notification obligations
- Emergency stop-work protocols

2.3 Closing the Gap

The National Agreement on Closing the Gap (2020) sets targets for improving outcomes for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples. Irongate acknowledges the Agreement and its underlying principle that "governments and the community controlled sector, together with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people, must change the way they work."

Our community benefit commitments (Section 4) are designed with reference to relevant Closing the Gap targets, particularly those relating to employment and economic participation.

SECTION 3: ENGAGEMENT APPROACH

3.1 Engagement Philosophy

Irongate is committed to building constructive relationships with Aboriginal communities and groups in the Pilbara. We recognise that the expansion of our operations has significant implications for the peoples who have lived on and cared for this Country for tens of thousands of years, and we take our responsibilities seriously.

Our engagement approach is guided by the following principles:
- Respect for Aboriginal culture, heritage, and connection to Country
- Open and honest communication
- Timely and adequate information sharing
- Genuine listening and responsiveness to concerns
- Commitment to delivering tangible benefits for communities

3.2 Key Aboriginal Stakeholders

The following Aboriginal groups have been identified as key stakeholders for the Stage 3 expansion:

a) Banyjima People: The Banyjima People are the determined native title holders for the majority of the expansion footprint. The Banyjima Traditional Owners Prescribed Body Corporate (PBC) is the registered native title body corporate for the group. Irongate has an existing ILUA with the Banyjima PBC for Stage 1 and 2 operations, which provides a foundation for Stage 3 negotiations.

b) Nyiyaparli People: The Nyiyaparli Yinhawangka Baiyungu Aboriginal Corporation RNTBC represents native title holders for the eastern portion of the expansion area. This is Irongate's first engagement with this group, as their native title determination post-dates our existing operational area.

c) Yinhawangka People: While not determined native title holders within the project area, the Yinhawangka People have asserted connection to Country in the broader region and have been engaged as a matter of best practice.

d) Martu People: The Martu People's native title area is adjacent to the project boundary. While not directly affected by the expansion footprint, they have been notified of the project and invited to raise any concerns.

3.3 Engagement Activities Completed

Since the Stage 3 concept was developed in early 2025, Irongate has undertaken the following engagement activities:

- Future Act notice process: Statutory notifications issued to Banyjima and Nyiyaparli PBCs (September 2025)
- ILUA negotiation meetings: Three meetings with Banyjima PBC negotiating team (July, August, October 2025)
- Initial meeting with Nyiyaparli RNTBC: One introductory meeting (September 2025)
- Community information sessions: Two sessions held at Jigalong community (28 and 62 attendees respectively)
- Desktop heritage assessment: Commissioned from external heritage consultancy, October 2025
- Letters notifying Yinhawangka and Martu groups of the project (August 2025)

3.4 Planned Engagement Activities

Prior to EIS lodgement:
- Continue ILUA negotiations with Banyjima and Nyiyaparli PBCs
- Conduct Level 3 heritage surveys with ACHCB representatives
- Hold further community information sessions in Jigalong and Tom Price
- Commission social impact assessment with community input component

During construction and operations:
- Quarterly community liaison meetings
- Annual community benefit reporting
- Heritage monitoring in accordance with CHMP

3.5 Limitations of Current Engagement

Irongate acknowledges that our engagement to date has been limited by the following factors:

- The Stage 3 expansion is a significant expansion of our operational footprint into areas where we do not have pre-existing relationships, particularly with the Nyiyaparli group.
- Our ILUA negotiation process is primarily a commercial and legal process. We recognise that it does not substitute for broader community relationships.
- Community information sessions have been informational rather than co-design processes. We acknowledge that we are presenting a development concept that has been determined internally for community comment, rather than designing the programme with community participation.
- We do not have consistent engagement with Elders and knowledge holders beyond the PBC and RNTBC governance structures. We recognise this as a gap.

SECTION 4: COMMUNITY BENEFIT COMMITMENTS

4.1 Employment and Training

Irongate commits to the following Aboriginal employment targets for the Stage 3 expansion:

- Construction phase: Minimum 12% Aboriginal workforce participation
- Operations phase: Minimum 15% Aboriginal workforce participation
- Apprenticeships and traineeships: Minimum 4 per year, with preference for Pilbara-based Aboriginal candidates

Our current operational workforce is 9.4% Aboriginal — below our stated 15% target. We acknowledge this and are investing in a new pre-employment pathway programme, developed in partnership with the North West Aboriginal Employment Network, to improve our performance.

4.2 Contracting

Irongate commits to:
- Engaging with Aboriginal businesses through our supplier diversity programme
- Targeting a minimum of 8% of Stage 3 procurement spend with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander businesses (by value)
- Providing business development support to Aboriginal businesses seeking to tender for project contracts

4.3 Community Development Fund

A Community Development Fund will be established, funded by a royalty-equivalent contribution of $0.40 per tonne of ore processed from the Stage 3 operation. Based on projected production, this is estimated to generate approximately $6.4M per year at full production.

Fund governance will be determined through the ILUA negotiation process. Irongate's preference is for a joint governance committee comprising community representatives and Irongate representatives, with community representatives holding the majority of votes on fund allocation decisions.

4.4 Heritage Protection

Beyond minimum legislative requirements, Irongate commits to:
- Implementing a Cultural Heritage Monitoring Programme with on-Country participation by Aboriginal knowledge holders
- Contributing $2M to the Banyjima Cultural Heritage Documentation Project
- Providing access to project areas for cultural maintenance activities during operational periods, subject to safety requirements

SECTION 5: DATA AND INFORMATION MANAGEMENT

5.1 Cultural Heritage Data

Cultural heritage data collected during Level 3 surveys will be managed in accordance with the CHMP and the requirements of the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2021. ACHCBs will receive copies of all survey reports and will have input into how heritage information is stored and accessed.

5.2 Social Research Data

Social research data collected as part of the social impact assessment will be managed in accordance with Irongate's Privacy Policy and the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth). De-identified data will be used for reporting purposes.

Irongate is aware of the Maiam nayri Wingara Indigenous Data Sovereignty principles and their application to data collected about Aboriginal communities. We are in the early stages of considering how these principles might be applied to our data management practices. This is an area requiring further development in consultation with the communities concerned.

SECTION 6: SOCIAL IMPACT ASSESSMENT SUMMARY

6.1 Potential Positive Impacts

- Employment and income for Aboriginal Pilbara residents
- Contracting opportunities for Aboriginal businesses
- Community Development Fund investments
- Heritage documentation and protection activities
- Improved infrastructure (roads) with potential community access

6.2 Potential Negative Impacts

- Disturbance of cultural heritage sites (residual risk despite mitigation)
- Increased traffic on roads used by Aboriginal communities
- Pressure on housing and community services in regional towns
- Dust and noise impacts on communities
- Water resource competition (borefield development)
- Social disruption associated with large fly-in fly-out workforce

6.3 Cumulative Impacts

The Stage 3 expansion occurs within a broader context of significant resource development in the Pilbara. Cumulative impacts on Aboriginal communities from multiple concurrent developments — including pressures on housing, social services, and cultural heritage — are acknowledged as a concern raised by community members in information sessions. Irongate has commissioned a regional cumulative impact study, findings expected Q2 2026.

SECTION 7: CONCLUSION

Irongate Resources recognises the significant cultural, historical, and legal significance of the Pilbara landscape to the Aboriginal peoples whose Country this is. We acknowledge that resource development on this Country requires us to meet a high standard of engagement, heritage protection, and benefit sharing.

We are not complacent about the gaps in our current approach. The limitations identified in Section 3.5 are genuine, and our commitment to improving them is reflected in the planned engagement activities and the governance provisions of the Community Development Fund.

We look forward to continuing our negotiations with the Banyjima and Nyiyaparli PBCs, deepening our relationships with the broader community, and delivering a Stage 3 expansion that represents a fair contribution to the Pilbara communities whose Country supports our operations.
