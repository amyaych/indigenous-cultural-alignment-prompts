INTRODUCTION

Coral Horizon Development Group (CHDG) is pleased to present the Environmental and Cultural Management Framework (ECMF) for the Bula Paradise Resort development. This framework has been developed as part of the development consent process for a boutique eco-resort on the southern coastline of Viti Levu, in the Tikina Namosi district.

The resort will comprise 24 bure-style villas, a central lodge and dining facility, a spa and wellness centre, and a cultural interpretation centre. The project is positioned in the premium eco-tourism segment and is designed to appeal to international travellers seeking an authentic Fijian experience combined with luxury amenity.

The Tikina Namosi area is home to iTaukei (Indigenous Fijian) communities whose mataqali (landowning clans) hold customary title to the land on which the resort will be developed. The land for the resort has been obtained through a negotiated lease arrangement with the relevant mataqali through the iTaukei Land Trust (TLTB).

This framework sets out how CHDG will manage the environmental and cultural dimensions of the development in a manner that respects the Vanua — the living connection between the iTaukei people and their land, sea, and cultural heritage.

SECTION 1: UNDERSTANDING THE VANUA

1.1 What is the Vanua?

CHDG recognises that the land on which this development is proposed is not merely a physical asset. For the iTaukei communities of Tikina Namosi, the land is the Vanua — a living, relational concept that encompasses the physical land, the people who belong to it (the koro, villages), the ancestors, the spiritual connections, and the obligations of care and stewardship that bind them together.

The Vanua is not a concept that can be understood from outside. Our team has spent time with community members and consulted a cultural advisor to develop an introductory understanding, but we acknowledge that the Vanua's full meaning and significance lies with the people who live within it.

We understand our obligations in relation to the Vanua as follows:
- We must not damage or diminish the Vanua — physically, culturally, or spiritually
- Our use of the land must be in accordance with the wishes and authority of those who belong to the Vanua
- The benefits of the resort must flow back to the communities of the Vanua in meaningful, ongoing ways

1.2 Mataqali Authority

The mataqali is the primary landowning and governance unit in iTaukei society. The land for this project is held by two mataqali: Mataqali Navilawa and Mataqali Qolikoro. CHDG has entered into a lease arrangement with both mataqali through the TLTB.

We acknowledge that the lease arrangement, while legally appropriate, is only the beginning of our relationship with these communities. The signing of a lease does not confer permission to disregard the authority, protocols, and aspirations of the mataqali. We understand that ongoing relationship and responsiveness to community concerns is what makes our presence on this land legitimate.

1.3 Traditional Leadership

The paramount chief (Ratu) of Tikina Namosi has been formally approached and has provided a letter of no objection to the development. We acknowledge that the support of traditional leadership is essential, not merely ceremonial, and we commit to maintaining respectful relationships with traditional leaders throughout the project.

SECTION 2: COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT

2.1 Talanoa Process

CHDG has engaged with the communities of Tikina Namosi through a process that incorporates Talanoa — a Pacific dialogue approach built on relationships, storytelling, and the gradual building of shared understanding. We acknowledge that our use of the term Talanoa must be matched by genuine practice.

Talanoa sessions conducted to date:
- Koro of Namosi: Three sessions (February, May, August 2025), approximately 40–65 community members per session
- Mataqali Navilawa members: Two dedicated sessions (March, July 2025)
- Mataqali Qolikoro members: Two dedicated sessions (April, August 2025)
- Women's group sessions: Two sessions facilitated by a Fijian woman cultural advisor (June, August 2025)
- Youth group sessions: One session (July 2025)

2.2 What We Heard

Through these Talanoa sessions, community members raised the following themes consistently:

Positive aspirations:
- Employment for community members, especially youth, who currently leave the village for work
- Improved access to the internet and communications infrastructure
- Support for the local school
- Opportunities for women to earn income through traditional crafts and food production
- Pride in having their culture and traditions respected and shared with visitors

Concerns:
- Previous developers have made promises and not kept them
- Worry about outsiders changing the culture
- Concern about alcohol being brought into the area (several Namosi villages are alcohol-free)
- Questions about who would control the cultural interpretation centre and how Fijian culture would be represented
- Environmental concerns about water use and waste management
- Who will decide if something goes wrong?

2.3 How We Have Responded

Employment: We commit to a minimum 70% iTaukei workforce from Tikina Namosi communities (see Section 4).

Alcohol policy: The resort will operate as alcohol-free in the communal areas of the resort grounds adjoining community spaces, in accordance with community wishes. Villas will have a minibar option available, but alcohol will not be visible or accessible in communal areas used jointly with community members.

Cultural interpretation centre: Governance of the cultural interpretation centre will be held by a community-appointed Cultural Heritage Committee. CHDG will fund the centre, but the community controls what is shown and how culture is represented.

Infrastructure: The resort's communications infrastructure will be designed to include community access points in Namosi village.

Dispute resolution: An independent dispute resolution process has been established (see Section 5.3).

2.4 Limitations of Our Engagement

We acknowledge the following limitations in our engagement process:

- Our Talanoa sessions have been led in part by CHDG-engaged facilitators, not entirely by community facilitators. We recognise this affects the authenticity of the Talanoa space.
- We have not engaged sufficiently with the elders who are less mobile and less likely to attend village meetings. Additional elder-specific engagement is planned for Q4 2025.
- Our engagement has been primarily with the communities directly adjacent to the site. We have not engaged systematically with other villages in Tikina Namosi who may have connections to the area.
- The Talanoa process has informed our design but the core development concept was determined by CHDG before the Talanoa process began. A more genuine Talanoa would have started with the concept stage.

SECTION 3: CULTURAL HERITAGE MANAGEMENT

3.1 Cultural Heritage Sites

A cultural heritage survey was conducted in partnership with community elders and members of Mataqali Navilawa and Mataqali Qolikoro. The survey identified six sites of cultural significance within the development footprint:
- Two historic yavusa gathering areas
- One ancestral burial site
- One spiritual site associated with traditional practices
- Two historical habitation areas with artefacts

All six sites will be excluded from the development footprint and protected by a 30-metre exclusion zone. Any ground disturbance within 100 metres of a heritage site requires prior consultation with the Cultural Heritage Committee.

3.2 Cultural Interpretation Centre

The Cultural Interpretation Centre will be funded and built by CHDG but governed by a community Cultural Heritage Committee comprising:
- Three members nominated by Mataqali Navilawa
- Three members nominated by Mataqali Qolikoro
- Two members nominated by the wider Namosi community
- One CHDG liaison (non-voting, administrative support)

The Cultural Heritage Committee has authority over:
- What cultural content is displayed
- How iTaukei cultural practices are described and interpreted
- Which cultural activities are offered to guests
- Which community members are employed in cultural roles
- Whether and how photographs or recordings of cultural activities may be used

Intellectual Property: All cultural content, performances, designs, and traditional knowledge incorporated in the resort experience is the intellectual property of the relevant mataqali and community. CHDG has a non-exclusive licence to use this content within the resort only. Any commercial use beyond the resort requires written consent.

3.3 Sevusevu Protocol

All guests will participate in a formal sevusevu (traditional welcome ceremony) upon arrival. This will be conducted by a mataqali-appointed cultural host. CHDG recognises that sevusevu is a serious cultural obligation and protocol, not merely an entertainment activity. The ceremony will be treated accordingly.

SECTION 4: COMMUNITY BENEFIT

4.1 Employment

CHDG commits to the following employment targets:
- Total resort workforce: 68 positions (full-time equivalent)
- Minimum 70% (48 positions) from iTaukei communities in Tikina Namosi
- Senior management: CHDG will actively develop iTaukei staff into supervisory and management roles with a target of 40% iTaukei management representation within five years

A pre-employment training programme will be funded by CHDG and delivered in partnership with the Fiji National University in Namosi.

4.2 Revenue Sharing

In addition to land lease payments to the TLTB, CHDG commits to a community benefit contribution of:
- 3% of annual resort revenue to a Community Development Fund
- Governance of the Fund: managed by a jointly appointed committee with community representatives holding the majority of votes
- Fund priorities to be determined by community through annual consultation

4.3 Economic Integration

- Fresh produce supply: CHDG will source a minimum 40% of its fresh produce from community gardens and smallholder producers in Tikina Namosi
- Craft and product sales: A dedicated market space will be provided for community artisans and food producers to sell products directly to resort guests (no commission to CHDG)
- Cultural activities: Fees for cultural activities offered to guests (including guided walks, kava ceremonies, craft workshops) will go directly to the community members leading those activities

SECTION 5: ENVIRONMENTAL MANAGEMENT

5.1 Marine and Coastal Environment

The resort fronts a protected coral lagoon. CHDG has commissioned a coral reef assessment and will develop a Marine Protected Area management plan in partnership with community members who have traditional knowledge of the marine environment.

Water sports and guest activities in the lagoon will be subject to guidelines developed with community input. Traditional fishing rights of the mataqali are explicitly recognised and protected — any resort-related activities that conflict with traditional fishing practices will require negotiation and community consent.

5.2 Water and Waste

Water: The resort will use a combination of rainwater harvesting and a community shared borehole. Water use protocols have been developed to ensure resort operations do not compromise community water supply. The community has priority access to the water system.

Waste: A zero-to-landfill commitment has been made, with waste management solutions prioritised that create community employment (composting, recycling sorting) and whose outputs benefit the community (compost for community gardens).

5.3 Dispute Resolution

A two-stage dispute resolution process has been established:
1. Direct discussion between community representatives and CHDG resort management (target resolution within 14 days)
2. If unresolved, referral to an independent mediator acceptable to both parties

Community members may raise concerns through village chiefs, the Cultural Heritage Committee, or directly with the resort manager. CHDG commits to taking all concerns seriously and responding in writing within 7 days.

SECTION 6: MONITORING AND REVIEW

This framework will be reviewed annually in conjunction with the community Cultural Heritage Committee and a broader community consultation. The annual review will assess:
- Employment and economic benefit delivery
- Cultural heritage protection
- Community satisfaction
- Environmental performance
- Compliance with commitments in this document

Community members will be able to provide input through village meetings, through the Cultural Heritage Committee, and through an anonymous feedback mechanism. Reviews will be documented and made available to all community members.

CLOSING STATEMENT

Coral Horizon Development Group enters this development with a genuine desire to build something that is good for the communities of Tikina Namosi, good for Fiji's tourism industry, and good for the Vanua. We recognise that good intentions are not sufficient — only sustained, accountable action will demonstrate whether our commitment is real.

We commit to being held to account by the communities we are working with.

Vinaka vakalevu.
