INTRODUCTION

"Aaniin Mino-Bimaadiziwin" means, in Anishinaabemowin: "Hello, Good Life." The name is intentional. This programme begins with relationship — aaniin, a greeting — and centres the aspiration that all children, youth, and families in our communities deserve a good life, mino-bimaadiziwin, grounded in culture, language, land, and love.

This framework has been developed over 18 months of community-level dialogue, Elder guidance, and inter-community collaboration. It is not a proposal written by one organisation for others to receive. It is a collectively authored plan, driven by the communities it serves.

SECTION 1: FOUNDATION — RIGHTS, RECOGNITION, AND RECONCILIATION

1.1 The Distinction That Must Be Made

This programme involves three distinct Indigenous communities and their members. We are explicit about this because the differences are not superficial — they have profound implications for governance, legal rights, and cultural protocols.

Sagamok Anishnawbek First Nation and Wikwemikong Unceded Indian Reserve are First Nations communities with distinct identities, governance structures, and rights under Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982. Their members hold rights as "Indians" under the Indian Act (however deeply inadequate and colonial that Act is), and more fundamentally as the original peoples of this land under their own laws and under international human rights instruments including UNDRIP.

Métis Nation of Ontario Region 12 members are Métis people, a distinct Aboriginal people in Canada with their own unique culture, heritage, language (Michif), and rights affirmed by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Powley (2003) and more recently affirmed in R. v. Hirsekorn and through the broader Métis harvesting rights jurisprudence. Métis rights are not derivative of First Nations rights. They exist independently.

This programme does not merge or blur these distinctions. Each community has its own stream of programming, its own governance representation, and its own community-defined outcomes. What we share is geography, a history of resilience, and the vision of mino-bimaadiziwin.

1.2 UNDRIP and Bill C-15

Canada's endorsement of the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) and the enactment of the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act (Bill C-15, 2021) provide the international and domestic rights framework within which this programme operates.

Key UNDRIP articles that inform our programme design:

- Article 7: The right of Indigenous individuals to life, physical and mental integrity, liberty, and security.
- Article 12: The right to maintain, protect, and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and cultural expressions.
- Article 14: The right to establish and control educational systems and institutions in their own language.
- Article 23: The right to determine and develop priorities and strategies for exercising the right to development.
- Article 24: The right to their traditional medicines and to maintain their health practices.

The programme's design process — led by community members, grounded in community priorities, governed by community-appointed representatives — is an expression of Article 23.

1.3 Truth and Reconciliation — Calls to Action

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission's 94 Calls to Action (2015) provide a specific mandate for action. This programme directly addresses:

- Call to Action 61: Provide meaningful opportunities for culturally appropriate services, measures and supports for survivors of residential schools and their families and communities.
- Call to Action 65: Establish mechanisms to measure and report on progress regarding child and family services.
- Calls to Action 72-76 (Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls): The programme incorporates safety planning and family support components informed by the MMIWG Calls for Justice.

We acknowledge that citing the Calls to Action in a programme document is not the same as implementing them. Our accountability section (Section 6) describes how we will measure and report on our contribution to reconciliation in practice.

1.4 Jordan's Principle

Jordan's Principle — the child-first principle that arose from the tragic case of Jordan River Anderson, a First Nations child who died in hospital while governments disputed jurisdiction over his care — is embedded in this programme's design. No child will be denied services on the basis of government jurisdictional disputes. Where such disputes arise, the programme will fund the services needed and pursue cost recovery through separate administrative processes.

SECTION 2: COMMUNITY GOVERNANCE AND SELF-DETERMINATION

2.1 Programme Governance Structure

The programme is governed by the Mino-Bimaadiziwin Community Governance Circle (CGC), comprising:

First Nations representatives:
- Two representatives from Sagamok Anishnawbek First Nation (nominated by Band Council through community consultation)
- Two representatives from Wikwemikong Unceded Indian Reserve (nominated by Band Council through community consultation)
- Two Elder advisors (one from each First Nation community), who hold a non-voting advisory role with the right to pause proceedings for cultural reasons

Métis representative:
- Two representatives from Métis Nation of Ontario Region 12 (nominated by the MNO Region 12 Citizens Council)
- One Métis Elder advisor

Non-Indigenous partner:
- One representative from Community Wellness North (non-voting, administrative support role)

Decision-making: Governance Circle decisions are made by consensus among the Indigenous representatives. Community Wellness North has no decision-making authority over programme priorities, service design, or resource allocation.

The CGC is the highest authority in the programme. It sets programme priorities annually, approves budgets, hires the Programme Director, and reviews outcomes.

2.2 Community Advisory Councils

Each participating community has established a Community Advisory Council comprising Elders, parents, youth, service users, and cultural knowledge holders. These councils provide ongoing community input and have the authority to raise concerns directly with the CGC.

The Community Advisory Councils meet monthly. Their reports are standing items at CGC meetings.

2.3 OCAP® Compliance — First Nations Data

All data collection, management, and reporting for First Nations programme participants follows the OCAP® principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession), owned by the First Nations Information Governance Centre (FNIGC).

- Ownership: First Nations communities own data about their members.
- Control: Each First Nations community controls data collection and management within their community.
- Access: Community members have the right to access their own data.
- Possession: Physical custody of data rests with the communities, not with Community Wellness North.

Community Wellness North holds aggregate, de-identified data only for reporting to funders. Identifiable community-level data is held by each community's designated data custodian.

2.4 Métis Data Sovereignty

Data governance for Métis programme participants follows the specific principles developed by the Métis Nation of Ontario, informed by broader First Nations and Indigenous data sovereignty principles while being adapted to the Métis specific context. Key provisions include:

- MNO Region 12 owns data about its citizens
- Disaggregated Métis data is not shared with First Nations communities (and vice versa)
- All publications using Métis community data are reviewed and approved by MNO Region 12 before release

SECTION 3: CULTURAL PROGRAMMING

3.1 Land-Based Healing and Learning

At the core of Mino-Bimaadiziwin is the recognition that healing happens on the land. Disconnection from land is not only a spiritual and cultural wound — it is a direct outcome of colonial policies including reserve systems, residential schools, and child welfare interventions that removed children and families from their territories.

Land-based programming includes:
- Seasonal camps on traditional territories (minimum four per year per community)
- Weekly land-based learning circles for children and youth
- Trapping, harvesting, and food preparation programmes guided by community Elders and knowledge holders
- River and lake monitoring activities that combine western ecological science with traditional ecological knowledge

Cultural safety: All land-based programming is designed and led by community members. Community Wellness North staff do not facilitate land-based programming — they provide logistical and administrative support when requested.

3.2 Anishinaabemowin Language Revitalisation

Anishinaabemowin (Ojibwe) is a seriously endangered language. This programme incorporates language revitalisation as a health and wellbeing intervention, based on community-led research showing the correlation between language use and mental wellness outcomes.

Language activities include:
- Immersive language programming for children 0–5 (Nest model, in partnership with local language nest programmes)
- Elder-as-teacher sessions for families
- Language integration across all programme activities
- Support for Elders who are primary speakers to document and share their knowledge

Governance over language programming rests entirely with the communities and their designated language authorities. Community Wellness North has no role in language programme design.

3.3 Métis Cultural Programming

Métis cultural programming is governed by MNO Region 12 and is distinct from First Nations programming. Activities include:
- Métis jigging, fiddling, and beadwork
- Michif language exposure programming
- Voyageur and harvesting traditions
- Connections to the Métis Nation of Ontario's broader cultural calendar

The distinctness of Métis culture is respected. Programming does not blend Métis and First Nations cultural elements.

3.4 Cultural Safety — Service Environment

All programme services are delivered in culturally safe environments. What cultural safety means for this programme:

- Services are delivered in spaces controlled by communities (on-reserve community centres, cultural centres, Elders' lodge — not mainstream service offices)
- All programme staff undergo mandatory, ongoing cultural safety training developed and delivered by community members
- Staff who are non-Indigenous are in support roles, not leadership roles
- Smudging, ceremony, and other cultural practices are welcomed in all programme spaces
- All services are available in Anishinaabemowin upon request

SECTION 4: PROGRAMME SERVICES

4.1 Child and Family Support

The programme operates under the principle that healthy children require healthy families in healthy communities. Service design reflects this systemic understanding.

Core services:
- Home visiting for families with children 0–5
- Family strengthening circles (facilitated by community members, not counsellors)
- Crisis support and safety planning
- Connection to extended family and kinship networks (prioritised over institutional care)
- Support for families navigating child welfare systems, including advocacy

The programme explicitly aims to reduce the number of children entering the child welfare system by addressing root causes — poverty, housing instability, trauma, disconnection from culture — before crisis occurs.

4.2 Youth Programming

- Cultural mentorship programme connecting youth with Elders and knowledge holders
- Academic support with culturally grounded tutoring
- Leadership development
- Mental health support that integrates Indigenous healing approaches and Western clinical support where requested by the young person

4.3 Elder Wellbeing

Elders are centred as knowledge holders and community leaders, not merely as programme recipients. The programme:
- Pays Elders for their knowledge and teaching (compensation is set by each community)
- Provides practical support for Elders' wellbeing (transportation, home support, health navigation)
- Facilitates inter-generational connection as a core programme activity

SECTION 6: ACCOUNTABILITY AND EVALUATION

6.1 Community-Defined Outcomes

Outcomes for this programme were defined by the communities, not by the funder or Community Wellness North. Each community has developed its own vision of mino-bimaadiziwin and its own set of indicators for what that looks like in practice.

Common themes across community-defined outcomes:
- Children growing up knowing who they are, where they come from, and who their people are
- Families that feel strong enough to care for their own
- Elders feeling respected and connected
- Language alive in our communities
- Fewer children entering the child welfare system

These aspirational outcomes are complemented by measurable indicators developed with community input, including language use surveys, child welfare system interaction rates, and community-designed wellbeing assessments.

6.2 Evaluation Approach

The programme will be evaluated through a community-led evaluation process. Each community conducts its own annual evaluation, guided by protocols developed by its Community Advisory Council. A cross-community synthesis is produced by the Programme Director and reviewed by the CGC.

Evaluation methodology includes:
- Story-based evaluation (alongside quantitative measures)
- Elder and community member assessment panels
- Youth-designed self-assessment tools

External evaluation: An independent review will be conducted at the midpoint (Year 2) by a team that includes at least two Indigenous researchers, nominated with community input.
