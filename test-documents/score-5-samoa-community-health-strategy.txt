The Wellbeing of the Village
A Community-Led Health Strategy for Gagaifomauga District, Savai'i

LAUGA FAATALI — CEREMONIAL OPENING

This strategy was not written first. It was spoken first. It was spoken in fono (councils), in the fale (houses), under the breadfruit trees, and in the churches that stand at the heart of every village in Gagaifomauga. The writing came after, to capture what had already been said.

E pei o le vaa — we are like a canoe. The Va between us — the sacred relational space that holds us together — is what keeps us afloat. When the Va is tended carefully, we travel together. When it is neglected or broken, we are in danger. This strategy is, above all else, a commitment to Teu le Va — to tend the relational space between our people, our villages, and anyone who works among us.

Ia fa'afetai i le Atua mo ana foafoaga uma lava.

SECTION 1: KO LE MAFUA'AGA — THE ORIGIN OF THIS STRATEGY

1.1 Who Led This Work

This strategy was initiated by the Alii ma Faipule — the chiefly and oratorical leadership — of the twelve villages of Gagaifomauga District. The decision to develop a district health strategy was made at a district fono in February 2024, convened by the District Women's Committee's call to address the increasing rates of non-communicable diseases and the declining availability of health services in rural Savai'i.

The Soifua Samoa Health Alliance and the Pacific Health Foundation were invited to support the process. They did not lead it.

The strategy that follows belongs to Gagaifomauga. It was built by the people of these twelve villages across fourteen months of district and village fono, women's committee discussions, youth gatherings, and church-based health conversations. The external organisations provided resourcing, facilitation support where invited, and technical health knowledge when asked. All final decisions were made by the communities.

1.2 The Fa'amatai as Foundation

The Fa'amatai — the Samoan chiefly system — is the foundational governance structure of Samoan society. In the villages of Gagaifomauga, the Fa'amatai system organises land, labour, resources, relationships, and responsibility. It is not a historical artifact. It is the living authority through which communities govern themselves.

This strategy operates fully within the Fa'amatai system. Each village's contribution to the strategy was approved through its own village fono. The district strategy was endorsed by the district fono of Alii ma Faipule. All programme activities in individual villages require the consent and active engagement of village leadership.

External organisations working under this strategy do not have permission to deliver services in any village without the prior consent of that village's Alii ma Faipule. This is not a bureaucratic requirement — it is a fundamental expression of the Fa'amatai's authority.

1.3 The Church

In every village of Gagaifomauga, the church is a central pillar of community life, moral authority, and social organisation. The three main church communities of the district — LDS, Assembly of God, and Methodist — have co-endorsed this strategy, not as token signatories, but as active participants in its design and committed partners in its delivery.

Church networks reach every household. Church leaders carry moral authority that complements and, in some matters, exceeds formal governmental authority. Health programmes that work with the church, rather than around it, reach more people and earn deeper trust.

SECTION 2: TEU LE VA — THE RELATIONAL FOUNDATION

2.1 Understanding Va in This Strategy

Va is the sacred relational space between people, between communities, and between humans and the divine. Teu le Va — to tend, care for, and protect the Va — is a core ethical principle of Fa'asamoa.

In the context of this health strategy, Teu le Va means:

Between the strategy and the community: Every interaction between programme staff and community members must honour the Va. This means approaching communities correctly (through village leadership), speaking and listening with respect, and never placing the efficiency of a programme above the integrity of relationships.

Between external organisations and the Fa'amatai: The Va between outside organisations and village leadership is established through correct protocol — through seeking permission, through bringing appropriate gifts (koloa), through taking the time that relationships require. This process cannot be bypassed or abbreviated for operational convenience.

Between different communities in the district: The programme will not create competition or division between villages. Resources will be distributed equitably, decisions affecting multiple villages will be made through the district fono, and programme staff will be neutral in inter-village matters.

Between the living and the ancestors: Health, in Samoan understanding, is not separate from spiritual wellbeing. The programme acknowledges the connection between physical health and the spiritual dimensions of life that the church communities hold. We do not seek to manage this relationship — we seek to respect it.

2.2 What Teu le Va Requires of External Partners

The Soifua Samoa Health Alliance and Pacific Health Foundation have agreed to the following relational commitments:

- All engagement in villages begins with sevusevu and formal approach to village leadership
- No programme activity is announced or delivered without prior village fono approval
- Programme staff who are not Samoan will be accompanied by Samoan cultural liaisons in all community interactions
- External organisations will not speak for the community in public forums — community representatives speak for the community
- Programme reporting will be shared with village leadership before being submitted to funders
- If a village asks external partners to leave or pause their involvement, that will be respected without condition

SECTION 3: GOVERNANCE

3.1 District Governance Council

The strategy is governed by a District Governance Council (DGC) comprising:

- Two Alii and two Faipule representing each of the twelve villages (nominated through village fono)
- The Chairperson of the District Women's Committee
- The Chairperson of the District Youth Committee
- One representative from each of the three church groups
- Two observers from Soifua Samoa Health Alliance (non-voting)
- One observer from Pacific Health Foundation (non-voting)

The DGC is chaired by the senior Alii of the district, in accordance with traditional protocol. Decision-making follows fono protocols — discussion continues until consensus is reached. The external partner observers do not participate in discussions unless invited.

3.2 Village Health Committees

Each of the twelve villages has established a Village Health Committee (VHC) comprising women's committee representatives, youth representatives, church representatives, and a nominated Alii or Faipule. VHCs are accountable to their village fono and report to the DGC.

The VHC is the primary interface between the strategy and village households. No programme activity is delivered without VHC approval.

3.3 Women's Leadership

The District Women's Committee initiated this strategy and their leadership has been central throughout. Women's voices, which can sometimes be marginalised in fono settings, have been protected through dedicated women's fono and through the formal representation of the Women's Committee Chair on the DGC.

The programme explicitly acknowledges that women carry a disproportionate health burden in families and communities and are often the primary decision-makers for children's health. Any strategy that does not centre women's leadership is incomplete.

SECTION 4: HEALTH PRIORITIES

The following health priorities were identified through the community fono process. They are listed in the order of priority as determined by the communities, not by external health professionals.

Priority 1: Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)
Diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease are the leading causes of early death in the district. Community members named these as their primary concern. The programme will support community-led responses including:
- Village-based NCD screening (led by trained community health workers, not clinical staff from outside)
- Community gardens and food sovereignty initiatives, led by women's committees
- Physical activity programmes connected to traditional practices (fishing, farming, communal work)
- Cooking workshops reconnecting community members with traditional Samoan food as health food

Priority 2: Mental Health and Wellbeing
Youth mental health, grief, and the stress of economic pressure were raised consistently. Responses will include:
- Community-led peer support groups (Samoan language, led by trained community members)
- Elder-youth connection programmes
- Church-based pastoral counselling support and training
- Support for families affected by remittance pressure and economic stress

Priority 3: Maternal and Child Health
Access to quality antenatal and postnatal care, and infant nutrition, were identified particularly by women's committees. Responses include:
- Village-based midwife support and referral pathways
- Breastfeeding support circles led by experienced mothers
- Child health monitoring through VHC coordination

Priority 4: Access to Health Services
Geographic isolation and cost are significant barriers. Responses include:
- A district-level health transport arrangement (coordinated by VHCs)
- Telehealth facilitation for specialist consultations
- Medication access support

SECTION 5: CULTURAL DIMENSIONS OF HEALTH

5.1 Fa'asamoa and Health

The people of Gagaifomauga understand health through the lens of Fa'asamoa — the Samoan way. Health is not merely physical — it encompasses:
- Soifua mālōlōina (physical wellbeing)
- Agaga (spiritual wellbeing)
- Fealofani (relational wellbeing — being loved and caring for others)
- Fa'amaoni (integrity and living honestly)

The programme's health activities are designed to nourish all four dimensions. Programmes that address only physical symptoms without attending to relational and spiritual wellbeing are understood to be insufficient.

5.2 Traditional Healing

Traditional healers (fofo) continue to practice in the district and are trusted by many community members. The strategy takes a respectful, non-competitive approach to traditional healing — acknowledging its legitimacy, not seeking to displace it, and creating dialogue between traditional healers and health workers where community members invite it.

Traditional healers were consulted during the strategy development process and their perspectives are incorporated in the health priorities and approaches above.

5.3 The Church and Health

The church's role in health is not peripheral. Churches are physical spaces where communities gather, moral authorities whose endorsement shapes community behaviour, and networks through which health messages are communicated and normalised. Each of the three participating church groups has committed to:
- Integrating health education into church programmes where appropriate
- Providing physical space for programme activities
- Actively communicating programme activities to their congregations
- Supporting church members to participate in health activities

SECTION 6: DATA SOVEREIGNTY AND PRIVACY

6.1 Community Authority Over Health Data

Health data about the communities of Gagaifomauga belongs to those communities. This is not merely a principle — it is operationalised through specific data governance provisions developed by the DGC.

Key provisions:
- All identifiable health data is held by the VHC of the relevant village
- No data about community members is shared with external organisations or government bodies without explicit individual and VHC consent
- Aggregate district-level data is co-owned by the DGC and the external partners, and is shared only through a process approved by the DGC
- External partners have no right to publish findings from this programme without DGC approval

The DGC has reviewed and endorsed these provisions. They are not aspirational — they are binding conditions of participation for external organisations.

6.2 Research and Publication

Any research arising from programme activities must be approved by the DGC, must include Samoan researchers in meaningful roles, and must share findings with communities before external publication. Community members who contributed knowledge have the right to withdraw it from publication.

SECTION 7: ACCOUNTABILITY AND EVALUATION

7.1 Community-Determined Success

The communities of Gagaifomauga defined what success looks like for this strategy. The definition that emerged from fono was:

"When our grandchildren grow up strong in their bodies, clear in their culture, cared for by their village, and connected to God — that is success. When our young people do not have to leave to have a good life — that is success."

This framing guides the evaluation approach. Quantitative health metrics (NCD rates, screening rates, health service utilisation) are important and will be tracked. But they are not the whole measure of success.

7.2 Accountability to Communities First

The strategy is primarily accountable to the communities of Gagaifomauga. Reports will be presented to the district fono before being submitted to funders. Community members will be asked annually whether the programme is doing what they asked for, and whether external partners are behaving in accordance with the Va and the commitments they made.

Funders receive reports after communities do.

7.3 External Partner Accountability

External partners (Soifua Samoa Health Alliance and Pacific Health Foundation) are subject to an annual community-led performance review. The DGC will assess whether external partners have:
- Honoured the Va and the relational commitments they made
- Supported community leadership without usurping it
- Delivered the resources and support they committed to
- Treated community knowledge with appropriate respect

The DGC has the authority to end the partnership with external organisations if these commitments are not met.
